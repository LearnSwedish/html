The Subjunctions “om” and “ifall”

om and ifall both introduce conditional clauses and usually translate as “if”. However, ifall can sometimes mean “in case”, making it slightly more cautious or hypothetical in tone.

1. om — the neutral and most common form

om is the standard conditional subjunction. It expresses a clear condition — “if” in English — and is used in all types of Swedish, both spoken and written.

  • Du får följa med om du är snäll.
    You may come if you’re kind.
  • Jag går till stranden om det blir sol imorgon.
    I’ll go to the beach if it’s sunny tomorrow.
  • Vi hade tagit en annan väg om vi hade vetat vad som väntade.
    We would have taken another road if we had known what awaited us.

Register: neutral; suitable in both speech and writing.

2. ifall — more tentative or cautious

ifall also means “if”, but it often adds a sense of possibility, precaution, or uncertainty — similar to “in case” or “if perhaps” in English. It is common in spoken language.

  • Ta med ett paraply ifall det börjar regna.
    Bring an umbrella in case it starts to rain.
  • Ring mig ifall du blir sen.
    Call me if you’re late.
  • Vi kan stanna hemma ifall vädret blir sämre.
    We can stay home if the weather gets worse.

Register: informal, often spoken; slightly softer or more polite than om.

3. Comparison

Both words express conditions, but they differ in nuance:

  • om → neutral, straightforward condition (“if”)
  • ifall → tentative or precautionary condition (“in case”)

Example comparison:

  • Jag tar bussen om det regnar.
    I’ll take the bus if it rains.
  • Jag tar bussen ifall det regnar.
    I’ll take the bus in case it rains.

4. Summary

  • om — neutral and most common; expresses a direct condition.
  • ifall — informal and softer; expresses a possible or precautionary condition.

In most cases, both can be used interchangeably — but ifall adds a shade of hesitation or caution that om lacks.
