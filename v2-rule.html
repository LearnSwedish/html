Swedish Grammar

The V2 Rule
in Swedish

Word order and verb placement in main clauses

What is the V2 rule?

The V2 rule — short for verb-second — is a word order rule stating that the finite verb must always occupy the second position in a main clause. No matter what comes first in the sentence, the verb follows immediately after.

Subject first
Jag äter äpplet.
I eat the apple.
Adverb first
Idag äter jag äpplet.
Today I eat the apple.

Notice that when idag (today) moves to the front, the subject jag shifts to after the verb — so that the verb always stays in second position.

How other languages compare

The V2 rule is characteristic of the Germanic languages. English once had it too, but has largely lost it — keeping it only in a few formal or literary constructions.

  • English Today I eat the apple.
  • German Heute esse ich den Apfel.
  • Dutch Vandaag eet ik de appel.
  • Swedish Idag äter jag äpplet.

In German, Dutch, and Swedish, the verb moves to second position whenever the sentence begins with anything other than the subject. English does not enforce this — the verb stays in place.

The three key positions

It helps to think of a Swedish main clause as having three named positions. The V2 rule concerns the relationship between position 1 and position 2.

Position 1 — Front
Anything
Position 2 — V2
Finite verb
Position 3+ — Rest
Subject · objects · adverbs

Position 1 can be filled by the subject, a time adverbial, a place adverbial, an object, or almost any other constituent. Whatever is placed there, the finite verb must follow directly in position 2.

When the subject does not occupy position 1, it is pushed to position 3 — immediately after the verb. This is called inversion.

Inversion in practice

In English, moving an adverbial to the front of a sentence does not change the order of subject and verb: Today I eat the apple — the subject still comes before the verb.

In Swedish, moving anything other than the subject to position 1 triggers inversion: the subject and verb swap places so that the verb can remain in second position. This is the direct consequence of the V2 rule.

Position 1 Position 2 (verb) Position 3+ (rest)
Jag äter äpplet varje dag.
Subject Finite verb Object · adverbial
Varje dag äter jag äpplet.
Adverbial Finite verb Subject · object
Äpplet äter jag varje dag.
Object (topicalised) Finite verb Subject · adverbial

Notice that the finite verb äter occupies position 2 in all three sentences, regardless of what has been fronted.

A Note on Subordinate Clauses

It is important to note that the V2 rule is a main clause rule.

In subordinate clauses (sentences starting with words like att, eftersom, om), Swedish uses a different, more fixed word order where the V2 principle does not apply. In these cases, the subject always stays before the verb.

In summary

The core rule

In a Swedish main clause, the finite verb always occupies second position.

If anything other than the subject is placed first, the subject and verb swap places — inversion.

⚠ The V2 rule applies to main clauses only.
