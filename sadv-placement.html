Swedish Grammar

Adverbial Placement
in Swedish

Where to put words like 'inte', 'alltid', and 'aldrig'

The sentence adverbial

In Swedish, sentence adverbials (satsadverbial) are words that modify the meaning of the entire sentence. Common examples include:

Inte (not), alltid (always), aldrig (never), ofta (often), and sällan (seldom).

Positive
Jag dricker kaffe.
I drink coffee.
Negative (with 'inte')
Jag dricker inte kaffe.
I do not drink coffee.

Standard Position: Post-Verb

In a standard Swedish main clause where the subject comes first, the adverbial is placed immediately after the finite verb.

Subject Verb (Position 2) Adverbial Rest
Han äter aldrig frukost.
Vi reser ofta till England.

Unlike English (BE), where you say "I never eat", Swedish requires the verb to stay in the second position (the V2 rule), pushing the adverbial to the third spot.

Placement during Inversion

When the sentence starts with something other than the subject (triggering the V2 rule), the subject and verb swap places. In this case, the adverbial is placed after the subject.

Rule of Thumb: The adverbial always wants to be as close to the verb as possible, but it cannot "break" the bond between the verb and a fronted subject or the verb and an inverted subject.

Fronted (Pos 1) Verb (Pos 2) Subject Adverbial
Nu dricker jag inte
Förra året tränade han ofta

Subordinate Clauses: Closing the Gap

In subordinate clauses, the "gap" between the subject and the adverbial is closed.

While a Swedish main clause forces the verb between the subject and inte, a subordinate clause follows the same logic as English, German, and Dutch: the adverbial stays with the subject.

Swedish: ...eftersom jag inte dricker kaffe.

English: ...because I do not drink coffee.

German: ...weil ich nicht Kaffee trinke.

Dutch: ...omdat ik niet koffie drink.

In all these languages, the subject and the negation form a single unit in the subordinate clause. The V2 "interruption" only happens in Swedish main clauses.

In summary

The Adverbial Rule

1. In a Swedish main clause: The verb separates the subject and the adverbial (Jag dricker inte).

2. In a subordinate clause: The subject and adverbial stay together ("...att jag inte dricker"), just like in English, German, and Dutch.

💡 Think of the main clause as the "exception" where the verb pushes the adverbial away. In the subordinate clause, they return to their natural positions.
