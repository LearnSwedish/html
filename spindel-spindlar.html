Irregular plural patterns

Most Swedish plural forms follow regular and predictable rules, but a number of nouns behave irregularly. Below you will find the most important groups and how to handle them. Keep an eye on (1) possible stem vowel changes, (2) how the noun ends, and (3) whether the word is a loanword.

If you don't know what the word means, you only need to hover it.

Rule What to do
1. Vowel change + -er (hand → händer) Change vowel + -er
2. Vowel end (bakelse → bakelser) Just add -r
3. Vowel + l/r (fågel → fåglar) Drop last vowel + -ar
4. Loanwords |ium or |eum (museum → museer) Drop -um + -er
5. Highly irregular forms (öga → ögon) Must be memorised

Irregular plural
patterns

Most Swedish plural forms follow predictable patterns, but a number of nouns behave irregularly. Here you will find the most important groups to keep in mind.


  1. Watch for stem vowel changes
  2. Check how the noun ends
  3. Be aware of loanword patterns

If you don't know what the word means, just click on it.


The symbol | indicates “ends with”.


Rules

  1. Stem vowel change + -er (hand → händer, bok → böcker).
    Solution: change the vowel and add -er.
  2. Ends in a vowel → add -r (bakelse → bakelser, sko → skor).
    Solution: add -r.
  3. Ends in vowel + l/r → drop the final vowel (fågel → fåglar, sommar → somrar).
    Solution: remove the final vowel, then add the ending.
  4. Loanwords in |ium or |eum drop -um (museum → museer, laboratorium → laboratorier).
    Solution: drop -um and add -er.
  5. Highly irregular forms (huvud → huvuden, öga → ögon, öra → öron).
    Solution: must be memorised.
