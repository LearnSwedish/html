Pluralisation in Swedish

In Swedish, there are over 20 different ways to pluralise nouns. (Don’t be alarmed! In English, where pluralisation is often considered so simple because you usually just add an s [car » cars], there are, in fact, over 25 different pluralisation forms. Google it!) Among these, five methods account for over 97 percent of cases, with (1) the addition of -er being the most common, followed by (2) -ar, then (3) making no change at all (symbolised Ø), followed by (4) -or, and finally (5) -n. In roughly 75 percent of cases, there are clear pluralisation routines, while the choice between -er and -ar is open in about 25 percent of cases. (You just have to learn them.) However, when no other guidance is available, the rule applies: the shorter the word, the more likely you are to use -ar, and conversely, the longer the word, the more likely -er will apply.

Information

These figures are based on nouns from the 10,000 most commonly used words in Swedish. Among them, there are nearly 5,500 nouns, of which almost 500 are either singulare tantum (i.e., nouns that only occur in the singular, e.g. "marmor"/marble) or plurale tantum (i.e., nouns that only occur in the plural, e.g. "jeans"/jeans). Among the remaining 5,000 nouns, over 35 percent of words receive an -er ending in the indefinite plural form. To get there, in most cases, you simply need to add the -er ending to the singular form (sandal » sandaler), but sometimes other changes are required, such as altering a vowel (bok » böcker). Yes, our languages are truly a cultural creation!
