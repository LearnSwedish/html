Swedish Grammar

Definiteness
in Swedish

A structural overview with cross-linguistic comparison

What is definiteness?

Definiteness marks whether we are talking about something specific and identifiable, or something general and unspecified.

Indefinite
a car
any car — not yet identified
Definite
the car
a particular car — known or already mentioned

How other languages do it

Most European languages mark definiteness with a separate article placed before the noun. Swedish takes a different path.

  • English the car
  • German das Auto
  • Dutch de auto
  • Swedish bilen

In the Scandinavian languages — Swedish, Danish and Norwegian — definiteness is expressed by adding a suffix to the noun itself, rather than placing a separate article before it.

Gender determines the suffix

Swedish nouns belong to one of two genders: the en-gender or the ett-gender. The gender of a noun dictates which definite suffix it takes.

en-gender
en bil  →
bilen
ett-gender
ett hus  →
huset

To form the definite correctly, you must know a noun's gender — it determines the ending.

The twist: double definiteness

In English, adding an adjective is simple: the red car — article + adjective + noun. German and Dutch work identically: das rote Auto, de rode auto.

You might expect Swedish to follow the same logic — the suffix already marks definiteness, so an adjective would just slot in before it. But Swedish is not that straightforward. Instead, it requires double definiteness: both a definite article (den, det, de) and the definite suffix on the noun.

It is as if Swedish has a hidden "the" that surfaces the moment an adjective enters the phrase.

Definite article Adjective Noun + definite suffix
den röda bilen
1st marking 2nd marking

In summary

The core rule

Without an adjective, only the suffix is needed:

bil → bilen  ·  hus → huset

⚠ When an adjective is present, Swedish requires both a definite article (den / det / de) and a definite suffix on the noun — double definiteness.
