The Subjunctions “fastän” and “trots att”

fastän and trots att both express concession — a contrast between expectation and fact. fastän is neutral, while trots att often carries a more emphatic or subjective tone.

1. fastän — neutral and common

fastän introduces a subordinate clause that contrasts two ideas or facts. It corresponds to “although” or “even though” in English and is used in both speech and writing.

  • Han gick till jobbet fastän han hade sovit dåligt.
    He went to work although he had slept badly.
  • Hon hjälpte honom fastän hon var trött.
    She helped him although she was tired.
  • Vi satt ute fastän det var lite kallt.
    We sat outside although it was a bit cold.

Register: neutral; used in all styles of Swedish. It may sound slightly more formal than trots att in everyday speech.

2. trots att — emphatic or subjective

trots att also expresses concession, but often adds emphasis or the speaker’s attitude. It highlights the unexpectedness of the second clause compared with the first.

  • Han återvände till huset trots att taket stod i lågor.
    He returned to the house even though the roof was on fire.
  • De fortsatte spela trots att scenen höll på att rasa.
    They kept playing even though the stage was collapsing.
  • Hon log trots att hon var ledsen.
    She smiled even though she was sad.

Register: common in spoken Swedish; sounds more emotional or subjective than fastän.

3. Comparison

Both introduce a contrast between what might be expected and what actually happens, but differ in tone:

  • fastän → neutral, factual contrast (although)
  • trots att → emphatic or emotional contrast (even though)

Example comparison:

  • Hon gick till jobbet fastän hon var sjuk.
    She went to work although she was ill.
  • Hon gick till jobbet trots att hon var sjuk.
    She went to work even though she was ill.

4. Summary

  • fastän — neutral; expresses a simple contrast between two facts.
  • trots att — more emphatic or emotional; highlights the speaker’s viewpoint or surprise.

Both correspond to “although” or “even though” in English, but trots att sounds stronger and more personal.
