The Subjunctions “tills” and “till dess (att)”

tills and till dess (att) both express a time limit — something continues up to the point when something else happens. They correspond to “until” in English.

1. tills — the common, everyday form

tills is the standard subjunction meaning “until”. It introduces a clause describing an event or condition that marks the end of another action.

  • Vänta här tills jag kommer tillbaka.
    Wait here until I come back.
  • Han arbetade tills solen gick ner.
    He worked until the sun went down.
  • Vi stannade kvar tills filmen var slut.
    We stayed until the film was over.
  • Hon sjöng tills rösten inte bar längre.
    She sang until her voice could no longer carry.
  • Barnen lekte ute tills det blev mörkt.
    The children played outside until it got dark.

Register: neutral and very common; used in all styles of Swedish.

2. till dess (att) — formal or literary variant

till dess (att) has the same meaning as tills, but is more formal and less frequent in modern speech. It is often used in writing or to give a slightly solemn tone.

  • Vänta här till dess att jag kommer tillbaka.
    Wait here until I come back.
  • Han arbetade till dess att solen gick ner.
    He worked until the sun went down.
  • Hon stannade till dess att gästerna hade gått.
    She stayed until the guests had left.

Register: formal or literary; more common in written Swedish or older texts.

3. Comparison

Both subjunctions mean “until” and indicate that something continues up to a certain time point. The difference is mainly in formality and frequency:

  • tills → neutral and most common in everyday use.
  • till dess (att) → formal or literary; used for emphasis or stylistic variation.

Example comparison:

  • Vi väntade tills tåget kom.
    We waited until the train arrived.
  • Vi väntade till dess att tåget kom.
    We waited until the train arrived. (formal)

4. Summary

  • tills — neutral, common; used in both speech and writing.
  • till dess (att) — formal or literary; less frequent in modern speech.

Both correspond to “until” in English, but tills is the natural choice in everyday Swedish, while till dess (att) adds a formal or elegant tone.
