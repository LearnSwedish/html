Approximately 71.3% has it

Europe is home to around 750 million people, and 71.3% of them speak a language with two or more grammatical genders. The remaining 28.7% speak languages with zero grammatical gender — a group that includes English, Finnish, Hungarian, Estonian and Turkish. Within the group of languages with multiple genders, 54% have two genders (such as French, Italian, Dutch and Swedish). The language in Europe with the most grammatical genders for nouns is Latin, which historically had five genders.

When and why did English
lose its gender system?

English once had a full gender system (masculine, feminine, neuter). Between the 10th and 13th centuries, this was lost. Key factors: (1) Viking invasions: Old Norse mixed with Old English, simplifying grammar. (2) Norman Conquest (1066): The Norman French further eroded gender distinctions. (3) Island effect: England’s isolation intensified these shifts. By the 16th century, English used gender only in personal pronouns ("he," "she," "it").

The future?

Language evolution over time tends to move towards simplification, particularly when it comes to grammatical systems, like gender. If simplification trends continue, and it certainly will, and nowadays at an accelerated pace due to globalization and cultural shifts, then grammatical gender will eventually be fully eliminated in all European languages. A professional guess would be that this will be a fact by around year 2300.

Non-neuter and Neuter Gender

In this textbook, the terms "non-neuter gender" and "neuter gender" are used to explain the two noun genders in Swedish. But these terms have some issues. "Non-neuter gender" is especially problematic because it’s not even the most widely used term in English (which is "common gender"). Furthermore, neither term, even the more widely used "common gender", is all that helpful since they don’t really give you a clue about which gender they refer to. That's why, in everyday Swedish, people — teachers in regular schools included — prefer to use the simpler terms "en-ord" (en-words) and "ett-ord" (ett-words). This table is meant to make things a little clearer, and it also includes some examples. At the bottom, I’ve added a brief section for anyone interested in learning more about the historical development of Swedish gender.

Note: As a good rule of memory, remember that the expression "non-neuter noun" actually contains five "N"s. It should give anyone – even people who do not spend their daily lives pondering language but perhaps instead spend their waking hours lacing shoes or painting lines on our roads, or anything of a similar nature – at least a reasonably clear sense of some kind of favouritism towards the letter N, and this happens to be a very fortunate circumstance, for when you see that expression, the author or speaker is indeed referring to an "en"-word.


History

Swedish noun gender was more complex historically, with three grammatical genders: masculine, feminine and neuter. Over time, the masculine and feminine genders merged into the en-word gender while the ett-word gender (neuter) remained distinct. This transition occurred gradually during the 17th and 18th centuries. Despite this merger, some nouns are still treated as feminine in specific contexts. For example, the word klocka (clock) is sometimes referred to using the pronoun hon (she), as in "Hon är kvart över sju" ("She is quarter past seven"). Another notable example can be found in formal or literary language, where människa (human being) is still referred to with hon, as in "Människan är ett socialt väsen och hon utvecklas genom interaktion med andra." ("The human being is a social creature, and she develops through interaction with others."). The first major Swedish public text where only two grammatical genders are explicitly used, is the Swedish Bible translation of 1541, commonly known as the Gustav Vasa Bible. This translation marked an important step in the standardization of the Swedish language.
